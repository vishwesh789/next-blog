ACCOUNTANT EXPLAINS: Are Your Money Habits Keeping You Poor?

Achieving financial freedom is a dream that most of us have but only a few are able to fulfill. Often, we find ourselves stuck in a cycle of bad money habits that limit our ability to save and invest, and hold us back from reaching our financial goals.

In this article, we will look at some of the most common bad money habits that people have and provide tips on how to break out of them.

  1. Paying Yourself Last

One of the most common bad money habits is paying yourself last. Most people pay their bills as soon as they get paid and then save whatever is left over, if anything. However, this is a poor person’s habit. The rich pay themselves first. They take at least 10% of their income and put it into savings before they pay their bills. By doing this, they guarantee that they will save money and not just spend it all. This is important because people who pay themselves last often find that there is never anything left to save.

To break out of this habit, you need to make saving a priority. Treat it like paying a bill. Set up an automatic transfer to your savings account so that you don’t have to remember to do it every time you get paid. When you pay yourself first, you will find that you have to be more mindful of your spending and budget more effectively. You will also be less likely to overspend because you will have less money available to spend.

  1. Getting Comfortable with Bad Debt

Another bad money habit is getting comfortable with bad debt. Many people use credit cards to buy things they can’t afford and then carry a balance from month to month. This is a dangerous habit because credit card debt can quickly spiral out of control. Credit card companies make money from people who carry a balance because they charge high interest rates.

To break out of this habit, you need to be more mindful of your spending. Don’t buy things you can’t afford. If you can’t pay for something in cash, don’t buy it. Only use credit cards for emergencies or for things that you know you can pay off in full at the end of the month. If you have high-interest debt, prioritize paying it off as soon as possible.

  1. Not Having a Stockpile

Having a stockpile of cash is important because it gives you a buffer in case of emergencies. Most financial experts recommend having at least three to six months’ worth of living expenses saved up. This way, if you lose your job or have an unexpected expense, you won’t have to rely on credit cards or loans to get by.

To build up your stockpile, start by paying yourself first. Take at least 10% of your income and put it into savings every month. Once you have built up your stockpile, you can start investing the additional money you save.

  1. Not Knowing Your Income or Expenses Properly

Many people don’t have a clear understanding of their income or expenses. They may have a general idea of how much they make and spend, but they don’t have a detailed budget or financial plan. This can lead to overspending and not saving enough for the future.

To break out of this habit, start by tracking your income and expenses. Use a budget tracker to keep track of how much money you make, how much you spend, and how much you save. Make sure to include your savings in your budget so that you are paying yourself first. Review your budget every three months to see where you can cut back on expenses and increase your savings.

  1. Having Expensive Hobbies

A lot of people like to shop, and I guess part of this is retail therapy. But again, marketing, social media and these multibillion-pound organizations love to tell us how much we need to spend our money and spend our cash instead of keeping it.

There’s nothing wrong with having hobbies, but when they are expensive hobbies, they can start to chip away at your financial goals. You don’t have to give up your hobbies altogether, but it’s important to assess how much you’re spending on them and make sure they are not taking over your budget.

One way to manage this is to set a monthly or yearly budget for your hobbies and stick to it. For example, if you like playing golf, set a budget for how much you will spend on golf-related expenses such as equipment, greens fees, and travel expenses.

Another way to manage this is to find cheaper alternatives to your hobbies. For example, instead of paying for an expensive gym membership, you can try working out at home or going for a run outside. Or instead of going out to eat at fancy restaurants all the time, try cooking at home or finding cheaper places to eat.

  1. Not Investing in Yourself

Investing in yourself is one of the best investments you can make. This can mean taking courses or classes to improve your skills and knowledge, or investing in your health by eating healthy and exercising regularly.

Investing in yourself will not only improve your quality of life, but it can also help you earn more money in the long run. By improving your skills and knowledge, you may be able to qualify for better job opportunities or earn a higher salary.

So, don’t be afraid to invest in yourself. Take that course, learn that skill, or join that gym. It will pay off in the long run.

  1. Ignoring Retirement Planning

Retirement may seem far away, but it’s important to start planning for it as early as possible. Many people ignore retirement planning until it’s too late, and they end up having to work well into their golden years.

To avoid this, start saving for retirement as soon as possible. One way to do this is to set up a 401(k) or IRA account and contribute to it regularly. If your employer offers a matching contribution, make sure you are contributing enough to take full advantage of it.

Also, be sure to regularly review your retirement plan and adjust it as necessary. As you get closer to retirement age, you may need to adjust your investments and savings strategy to ensure you have enough money saved to retire comfortably.

  1. Living Beyond Your Means

Living beyond your means is one of the most common bad money habits. It’s easy to get caught up in the cycle of spending more than you earn, especially when you are surrounded by people who are doing the same.

But living beyond your means can lead to a lifetime of debt and financial stress. To avoid this, you need to be honest with yourself about your income and expenses and live within your means.

One way to do this is to create a realistic budget and stick to it. Make sure you are not spending more than you earn and prioritize your spending based on your needs and financial goals.

  1. Not Seeking Professional Financial Advice

Not seeking professional financial advice is another common mistake people make when it comes to managing their finances. While it’s good to be self-sufficient and take control of your own financial situation, sometimes it’s important to seek the advice of a professional. This is especially true when it comes to complex financial decisions such as investing, estate planning, or retirement planning.

A financial advisor can provide valuable guidance on how to allocate your investments, how to minimize your taxes, and how to plan for your long-term financial goals. They can also provide an objective perspective on your overall financial situation and help you make informed decisions based on your individual needs and circumstances.

Of course, not all financial advisors are created equal, and it’s important to do your research and find a qualified professional who has your best interests in mind. Look for advisors who are certified by reputable organizations such as the Certified Financial Planner Board of Standards, and who have a track record of success working with clients in similar situations to yours.

Ultimately, seeking professional financial advice can help you avoid costly mistakes, optimize your financial situation, and achieve your long-term financial goals.
